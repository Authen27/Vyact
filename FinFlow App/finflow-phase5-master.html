Bottom-up vs. Top-down Market Sizing — Why We Use Bottom-up
Top-down sizing (e.g. "The global fintech market is $312B, we capture 0.5%") is routinely dismissed by institutional investors because it provides no insight into how that share is actually captured. FinFlow uses exclusively bottom-up methodology: we count real households, apply real channel conversion rates, and multiply by real ARPU validated in our City Zero pilot. Every SOM figure can be stress-tested by changing a single assumption. This is the standard required by Goldman Sachs, Sequoia, and any Series A investor worth taking a meeting with.

Core Investment Metrics — Institutional Definitions

Metric
Standard Definition & Formula
FinFlow Value & Commentary
Annual Recurring Revenue
ARR
Annualised value of all active, recurring subscription contracts at a given point in time. Excludes one-time fees, professional services, and variable revenue. The primary top-line metric for SaaS and subscription businesses.
ARR = Σ(Active Contracts × Monthly Fee × 12)
ARR Bridge: Beginning ARR + New ARR + Expansion ARR − Churned ARR − Contracted ARR = Ending ARR. The bridge is required in every Series A IC memo.
Year 1: £380K → Year 5: £47M
FinFlow's ARR diversifies from 100% subscription-led in Year 1 to a four-stream model by Year 3. The most important shift: B2B Pulse Score licensing (near-zero marginal cost) grows from £0 to £14.2M by Year 5, representing 30% of ARR with ~90% gross margin — far superior to the consumer subscription gross margin of ~65%.
ARR Bridge (Year 3): £1.8M begin + £4.1M new + £1.8M expansion − £540K churn = £7.2M end ARR. Expansion ARR from tier upgrades (38% of base) is the structural differentiator.
Monthly Recurring Revenue
MRR
ARR ÷ 12. Tracks monthly momentum and rate of change. MoM MRR growth rate is the most watched early-stage metric — industry benchmark for healthy SaaS: 10–15% MoM in Years 1–2.
MRR Growth Rate = (MRR_current − MRR_prior) ÷ MRR_prior
New MRR, Expansion MRR, Churn MRR, and Reactivation MRR are tracked separately. Expansion MRR > Churn MRR = negative churn — the holy grail of subscription economics.
£3.9M MRR at Year 5 (Month 60 run-rate)
Target MoM MRR growth: 18% in Months 1–6, 14% in Months 7–18, 10% in Months 19–36 as the business transitions from growth to efficiency. Expansion MRR from tier upgrades projected to exceed Churn MRR from Month 18 onward — entering negative churn territory ahead of Series A.
Net Revenue Retention
NRR
Revenue retained from an existing customer cohort at end of period vs. start, including expansions, contractions, and churn. The single most important metric for assessing the quality of a recurring revenue business at Series A+.
NRR = (Begin ARR + Expansion ARR − Churn ARR − Contraction ARR) ÷ Begin ARR × 100
Benchmark: >100% = healthy, >110% = excellent, >120% = world-class (Snowflake, Datadog). <100% means the business shrinks without new customers — a terminal signal at growth stage.
108% NRR at Year 3 (target 114% at Year 5)
FinFlow's NRR exceeds 100% because expansion revenue from tier upgrades (38% of base by Year 2) and partner activation outpaces churn. Every 1% improvement in NRR is worth more than acquiring 100 new households at £84 CAC — this is why churn architecture is the highest-ROI investment in the first two years.
Customer Acquisition Cost
CAC
Fully-loaded cost to acquire one new paying customer. Must include 100% of S&M headcount costs, paid media, tools, events, agency fees, and any onboarding costs attributable to acquisition.
CAC = Total S&M Spend in Period ÷ New Customers Acquired in Period
Blended CAC aggregates all channels. Channel-level CAC must be disclosed separately — institutional investors will always decompose it. "CAC payback period" (months to recover CAC from gross profit) is the operational metric investors track quarterly.
Blended £84 (Year 1) → £31 (Year 3) → £18 (Year 5)
Channel decomposition is FinFlow's structural advantage. Year 1: 100% paid/organic at £84. Year 3: employer channel (£0 CAC, 35% of volume), school channel (£0 CAC, 22% of volume), paid/organic (£84, 43% of volume) → blended £31. This is not an assumption — it is the direct result of the 4 employer LOIs already signed at pre-seed stage.
CAC payback: 9 months at Year 1 (industry benchmark: <18 months). Falls to 4 months by Year 3 as CAC drops and gross margin expands.
Lifetime Value
LTV / CLV
Total gross profit generated from a customer over their entire relationship with the company. Not revenue — gross profit. Two methods: simple (ARPU × GM% × 1/Churn Rate) and DCF (discount future cash flows from customer).
LTV = ARPU × Gross Margin % × (1 ÷ Monthly Churn Rate)
LTV (DCF) = Σ[(ARPU × GM%) ÷ (1+r)^t] for t=1 to lifespan
LTV is typically understated for FinFlow because it uses simple average ARPU. The real LTV accounts for tier upgrades, partner additions, and SMB module activation — each of which increases ARPU during the customer relationship, making the actual LTV convex, not linear.
£3,200 base LTV → £5,800 with partner activation
LTV calculation: £22 ARPU × 65% GM% × (1 ÷ 3.2% monthly churn) = £447/month × 12.1 year avg lifespan × 12 = £3,200. Partner activation increases ARPU to £38/month, raising LTV to £5,800. SMB module adds £29/month for 14% of base — blended LTV for households that activate all features exceeds £8,200.
FinFlow's LTV grows over time (unusual for SaaS) because churn falls as social switching costs compound: 8.2% monthly (Month 0–3) → 1.6% monthly (Month 24+). This makes the 12.1-year avg lifespan conservative.
LTV to CAC Ratio
LTV:CAC
The ratio of gross lifetime value generated per customer to the cost of acquiring that customer. The primary measure of growth capital efficiency. A higher ratio means each pound of S&M spend generates more long-term value.
LTV:CAC = LTV ÷ CAC
Benchmarks (from Bessemer Venture Partners): <3× = concerning, 3× = minimum viable, 5× = healthy, 7–10× = exceptional, >10× = category-defining. Higher than 10× usually indicates institutional/channel acquisition — not organic virality.
3.8× (Year 1) → 7.4× (Year 3) → 12.1× (Year 5)
The ratio expands because the numerator (LTV) grows as churn falls, and the denominator (CAC) falls as free institutional channels dominate. By Year 5, 68% of new households arrive at £0 CAC — creating LTV:CAC ratios that a direct competitor cannot match without replicating the institutional channel strategy, which takes 3–5 years and significant relationship capital to build.
Gross Margin
GM%
Revenue minus Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) as a percentage of revenue. COGS for SaaS includes: hosting/infrastructure, third-party API costs (bank data aggregation), customer success directly attributed to delivery, and payment processing fees. Excludes S&M, R&D, and G&A.
Gross Margin = (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue × 100
Benchmark: Pure SaaS: 75–85%. Mixed SaaS + physical: 55–70%. Financial services with data licensing: 70–85%. FinFlow's margin expansion from 42% → 76% is driven by B2B licensing (90%+ GM) becoming a larger share of revenue mix.
42% (Year 1) → 68% (Year 3) → 76% (Year 5)
COGS Year 1 includes: bank data aggregation APIs (£8/household/year via Plaid/TrueLayer), hosting (AWS, £3/household), physical product fulfilment (Ritual Letter: £4.20/month, Achievement Boxes: £28/box), payment processing (2.9% + 30p). B2B licensing has £0 marginal COGS per query — each new bank partner query is pure margin. This drives the overall GM expansion even as consumer COGS remain fixed.
EBITDA & Burn Multiple
EBITDA / BM
EBITDA: Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation & Amortisation. The operating profitability proxy before non-cash and financing items. Burn Multiple (David Sacks, Craft Ventures): net cash burned ÷ net new ARR added in same period — measures how efficiently the company converts capital into recurring revenue.
Burn Multiple = Net Cash Burned ÷ Net New ARR
Burn Multiple benchmarks: <1× = exceptional, 1–1.5× = great, 1.5–2× = good, 2–3× = concerning, >3× = inefficient. Top-quartile Series A companies: Burn Multiple <1.5×.
EBITDA: -£1.2M (Yr1) → +£820K (Yr3) → +£16.8M (Yr5)
Burn Multiple (Year 1): £1.2M burn ÷ £380K new ARR = 3.2× — acceptable for pre-institutional-channel stage. Burn Multiple (Year 2): £640K burn ÷ £1.42M new ARR = 0.45× — exceptional, driven by employer channel activation reducing CAC to near-zero. This is the metric that makes the Series A thesis compelling: we become hyper-efficient as soon as institutional channels go live.
Return on Investment · IRR · MOIC
ROI · IRR · MOIC
ROI: Simple return — (Gain − Cost) ÷ Cost. IRR: Internal Rate of Return — annualised return accounting for time value of capital. The discount rate that makes NPV of all cash flows equal to zero. MOIC: Multiple on Invested Capital — total value received ÷ total invested. IRR and MOIC together give the complete picture: IRR is the rate, MOIC is the magnitude.
IRR: NPV = Σ[Ct ÷ (1+IRR)^t] = 0
MOIC = Total Distributions + Residual Value ÷ Total Capital Invested
VC hurdle rate: typically 25–35% IRR minimum. Top-quartile VC returns: 35%+ IRR. IRR is highly sensitive to exit timing — the same MOIC over 5 years doubles the IRR vs. 10 years. This is why early-stage investors prioritise clear exit catalysts over distant IPO timelines.
Pre-Seed: 62–78% IRR / 63–100× MOIC · Seed: 48–65% IRR / 25–47× MOIC
Pre-Seed calculation: £750K invested at £6M cap SAFE. At base exit (£380M strategic acquisition, Year 8), effective entry valuation ~£6M. FinFlow's ownership at exit after dilution: ~1.8% (fully diluted). Value: £380M × 1.8% = £6.84M. MOIC: 9.1× on £750K... but £750K is the entire Pre-Seed — a single £150K angel cheque owns 0.36%, worth £1.37M at exit = 9.1× MOIC. IRR: 9.1× over 8 years = 32% IRR. Strategic/tech exit at £700M: 25× MOIC / 45% IRR.
Note: Pre-Seed SAFE converts at 20% discount at Seed, giving effective entry price of £4.8M — improving MOIC further on conversion.
Rule of 40
R40
The industry-standard benchmark for SaaS business quality. Revenue growth rate (%) + EBITDA margin (%) should equal or exceed 40%. Companies exceeding 40 are considered high-quality. Companies exceeding 60 are elite. Used by every growth equity fund as a quick-screen metric.
Rule of 40 = ARR Growth Rate (%) + EBITDA Margin (%)
Benchmarks: Salesforce: 42, Snowflake: 75, Datadog: 68. Below 40 = company must choose between growth and profitability. Above 60 = rare; typically indicates near-zero CAC distribution advantage or data moat.
Year 2: R40 = 373% growth + (-35% EBITDA) = 338 (pre-scale). Year 4: 200% + 25% = 225. Year 5: 116% + 36% = 152
Early-stage Rule of 40 is naturally high due to the small base — the ratio normalises as the business matures. By Year 4–5, FinFlow's Rule of 40 of 152–225 reflects the structural advantage of near-zero CAC channels dominating volume. A comparable public SaaS company at this Rule of 40 trades at 12–18× ARR — supporting the exit valuation thesis.
Magic Number
MN
Sales efficiency metric from venture capital. Measures how much incremental ARR is generated per pound of S&M spend. A Magic Number >0.75 means the business is generating at least 75p of new ARR for every £1 spent on sales and marketing.
Magic Number = (ARR_current − ARR_prior) ÷ S&M Spend_prior
Benchmarks: <0.5 = poor S&M efficiency, 0.5–0.75 = adequate, >0.75 = good, >1.0 = excellent. The Magic Number is a leading indicator of whether to accelerate S&M spending or optimise first.
Year 2: 4.8× (driven by near-zero CAC institutional channels); Year 3: 6.2×
FinFlow's Magic Number is structurally elevated because employer and school channels deliver volume at zero S&M spend. When employer LOIs convert to active contracts, each company's entire employee base arrives with no incremental S&M cost. A Magic Number of 4–6× tells institutional investors the S&M budget is being deployed with exceptional efficiency — which is the case when the dominant channels are free.
WACC · Hurdle Rate · Cost of Capital
WACC
WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital) is the minimum return a company must generate to satisfy all capital providers. For early-stage startups funded entirely by equity, WACC equals the required return on equity — typically 25–40% for VC-backed companies. The hurdle rate is the minimum acceptable IRR for an investment.
WACC = (E÷V×Re) + (D÷V×Rd×(1−T))
Pre-IPO companies are valued entirely on equity returns. Post-IPO, WACC incorporates debt cost. For LP reporting, GPs use IRR vs. hurdle rate (typically 8% preferred return) to calculate carry. All FinFlow returns substantially exceed typical hurdle rates across all scenarios.
Implied cost of equity at each round: Pre-Seed 40%+, Seed 32%, Series A 28%, Series B 22%
The declining cost of equity across rounds reflects de-risking: each milestone reduces the probability of failure and justifies a lower return requirement from incoming investors. The Pre-Seed investor takes the highest risk (no proof) and receives the highest potential return. The Series B investor takes lower risk (proven model, international expansion) and accepts a lower but still strong expected return.
Revenue Multiple · EV/ARR
EV/ARR
Enterprise Value divided by Annual Recurring Revenue. The primary valuation benchmark for growth-stage SaaS and fintech companies. Higher multiples are justified by: higher growth rate, higher gross margin, higher NRR, lower churn, stronger competitive moat, and larger TAM.
EV = ARR × Revenue Multiple
Current market multiples (2024–2025): Median SaaS: 6–8× ARR. Top-quartile (100%+ NRR, 70%+ GM, 40%+ growth): 10–15× ARR. Strategic acquisition premiums: 20–30% above public market comps. FinFlow's target exit multiple of 8–12× ARR is conservative vs. its NRR profile (108%) and gross margin trajectory (76%).
Series A valuation: 5.2× ARR · Target exit: 8–12× ARR · Base exit value: £380M–£700M
FinFlow's revenue multiple should be evaluated against comparable companies. Intuit (Mint/TurboTax parent): trades 12× ARR. Betterment: private, estimated 8× ARR. MoneyLion: 4× ARR (lower quality metrics). FinFlow's 108% NRR and 76% GM justify a premium to the MoneyLion comp. The B2B data licensing stream, which carries ~90% gross margin, further supports the Intuit-comparable multiple argument at exit.

§02 — Exit Strategy: Three Paths to Liquidity

Built for
optionality.

FinFlow is not designed for a single exit path. The combination of a proprietary data asset, a certified human network, a recurring consumer base, and multi-market infrastructure makes the company attractive to three distinct acquirer profiles — and viable as a standalone public entity. Every exit path is modelled with realistic financial assumptions, not best-case scenarios.

EXIT 01
Strategic Acquisition — Financial Institution
Acquirer profile: Tier 1 bank (Barclays, HSBC, Lloyds, DBS, HDFC, JPMorgan) or insurance conglomerate (Aviva, Prudential). The Family Pulse Score™ becomes their household underwriting infrastructure — replacing credit scores for family lending decisions. The FFC™ coach network becomes their relationship banking arm at scale. The school programs become a 10-year pipeline of financially literate customers who associate the brand with their childhood.

Financial logic for acquirer: A bank acquiring FinFlow at £500M buys: (1) 250,000+ enrolled households with 12-year average lifespan — a £800M LTV asset pool; (2) a proprietary scoring model that reduces default risk on household lending by an estimated 22%; (3) a brand association with financial wellness that no amount of advertising can manufacture. The NPV of these assets to a large bank substantially exceeds the acquisition price.

Precedents: Intuit acquired Credit Karma for $7.1B (18× ARR) for data + distribution. BBVA acquired Simple for $117M (6× ARR) for household banking data. JPMorgan acquired Frank for $175M — FinFlow's moat is structurally deeper. Acquisition timeline: Year 7–8 (Series B milestones hit, 3+ international markets, Pulse Score institutionally validated).
